Apheresis Device

Overview

Designing an interface for every skill level.

A client set out to build a next-generation, all-in-one apheresis device, combining the capabilities of several separate machines into a single system. Our team needed a GUI that worked for brand-new operators and 20-year veterans alike.

The interface needed to guide first-time users through unfamiliar medical procedures, while staying out of the way of experienced operators who just wanted speed and flexibility.

Because the device supports complex, multi-step blood collection procedures across variable environments — fixed sites, mobile blood drive buses, temporary setups — the experience had to simplify technical decisions without sacrificing accuracy or safety. The result was a flexible, research-validated interface that adapted to operator expertise and real-time procedure status.

The Challenge

This wasn't just a screen redesign.

The device replaced the separate functions of several existing machines with one unified system, introducing entirely new workflows for people who'd spent years on older, single-purpose devices.

—Many operators were transitioning from fragmented, manual processes to a single, guided digital workflow.
—Donors and time-pressured environments — in one case, a 45-minute total window at a high school blood drive — added constraints most software teams never have to design around.

The process moved through four phases — outlined below. Each step is linked in the bar above so you can jump straight to it.

Kickoff & Immersion

Aligning on scope, then mapping the workflow.

The kickoff aligned the design team with the client's stakeholders on a shared goal: design a new graphical interface for one collection protocol that could later scale into a full design system for the platform.

Working from stakeholder deep-dives across marketing, data systems, and account management, plus hands-on demos of current-generation devices, the team compiled a single task analysis and workflow map accounting for hardware, user actions, and external systems together, rather than designing the screen in isolation.

Discover

Understanding how operators work today.

Discovery centered on how operators actually use apheresis devices in the field today — the workarounds they rely on, the gaps in current interfaces, and what they expected from a next-generation system. These conversations, paired with a heuristic analysis of the existing interface, surfaced pain points across three groups:

—Operators juggling kits and tasks: manual, repetitive data entry; physical kit/tubing management; alarm fatigue; clunky end-of-procedure documentation.
—New operators, often without prior medical training — sometimes confusion selecting the right product/procedure, alarm confusion.
—Donors, indirectly affected — procedure reactions, vein/flow issues, and tight collection windows that limit how much time operators have to work with.
Define

Showing operators what's possible — without overwhelming them.

Defining the experience meant testing two fundamentally different navigation philosophies with real clinical educators and operators: a guided, linear concept, and a flexible, lateral one. Every operator regardless of experience level has their own way of installing a kit, checking donor history, and responding to alerts, so the interface had to support many valid paths rather than enforce one. Participants clearly preferred the lateral model, which became the blueprint for every screen that followed.

Key Features

The features that guide every operator through it.

Adaptive Guidance

Minimal instructions for experts, detailed step-by-step support for beginners.

Global Status

A color-coded, consistently placed status indicator that builds a shared mental model across every screen.

Lateral Navigation

Freeform navigation so operators can multitask across kit setup, procedure input, and live monitoring.

Clear Alerts

Concise troubleshooting steps paired with robust, intuitive alert states.

Run Screen Dashboard

A monitoring view optimized for legibility from across the room.

Deliver

Testing & handoff.

The final experience was validated across two additional rounds of research — a virtual evaluative study with external blood center operators, and an in-person/virtual formative usability study that benchmarked the design directly against current client and competitor devices. High-fidelity wireframes and an interactive, non-coded prototype supported both testing and developer handoff.
